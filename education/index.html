Module 01 4 min read

Creative Testing with Neon Blue

Understand why continuous creative testing remains the highest-leverage growth activity in a crowded channel landscape — and how AI changes the speed at which you can learn.

1.1

Why Creative Testing Matters

Every channel you operate in, whether that is email, push, ads or owned experiences is crowded. Users today are overwhelmed with messages from brands competing for their attention, and they have more alternatives than ever. Generic messaging gets ignored. Truly differentiating how you talk to your users and speaking to their problems and desires isn't a nice-to-have, it's non-negotiable.

The only reliable way to know what actually speaks to your users is through rigorous and continuous testing. What you think will work often doesn't. What works for one segment won't work for another. The more you test, the more you learn. The more you learn, the sharper your messaging gets. Across every customer touchpoint.

1.2

Why Creative Testing Has Been Capped

Creative testing isn't just one task — it's a chain of steps, each owned by different people, using different tools, on different timelines.

Generating and validating creative takes weeks. A single email variant involves a copywriter, a designer, and often a brand or legal reviewer. According to Litmus, most marketing teams historically needed two or more weeks to produce a single email — from brief to copy to design to QA to approval. Push notifications are faster to produce but still pass through the same approval chain. Ad creative adds another layer: platform-specific formats, compliance review, and often agency coordination. Landing pages bring in product, engineering, or web teams. For each variant you want to test, multiply that workflow. Testing six variants instead of two doesn't take three times as long — but it's close.

Analyzing results takes dedicated expertise and patience. Tests need to run long enough to reach statistical significance — typically two to four weeks depending on traffic volume. Then someone needs to pull the data, normalize across metrics, interpret what actually happened, and package it in a way that non-technical stakeholders can act on. Most teams don't have a dedicated analyst for this. It falls to whoever has bandwidth, which means it often doesn't happen rigorously — or at all.

Feeding learnings back into production is where most programs break down entirely. A test result is only valuable if it changes what you do next. That means updating templates, briefing the creative team, adjusting strategy, and documenting what was learned. In practice, results sit in a slide deck that gets reviewed once and forgotten. The next campaign starts from scratch instead of building on what you already know.

Now multiply all of that across channels. Email, push, ads, landing pages — each with its own platform, its own team, its own approval workflow. Running a structured testing program in one channel is hard. Running it across four simultaneously, with learnings flowing between them, is a full-time operation. Most marketing teams simply aren't staffed for it.

1.4

Working with Neon Blue, your team spends less time coordinating and more time deciding.

Less of: Back-and-forth with creative agencies. Chasing deliverables across channels. Sitting in dashboards trying to piece together what worked. Writing briefs, waiting for drafts, giving feedback, waiting again.

Get Started with Neon Blue

The tactical guide to connecting your data, launching your first test, and turning on the continuous testing engine across your channels.

2.1

Connecting Your Data

Neon Blue learns through testing — not through pre-built segments or asset libraries. We need your customer data and your existing creative, but the bar to get started is deliberately low.

Data: start with what you have. The base requirement is a connection to your ecommerce platform and your messaging platform — a one-click Shopify app install and a single Klaviyo API key. Setup takes about 15 minutes. That gives us user IDs, transaction history, product catalog, page view analytics, and email engagement — enough to launch experiments, learn, and deliver measurable lift. Over time, you can layer in behavioral analytics (Amplitude), attribution data, or a direct data warehouse connection (Snowflake) for richer signal. But none of that is required to get started.

Creative: we work with what's already there. No photoshoots, no new asset production. At the base level, we pull your product images and copy directly from your site and pair them with different messaging angles to generate dozens of testable variants from your existing catalog. If you want to go further, we can index your historical campaign assets, lifestyle photography, social content, and ad creative — but the base is where every successful pilot we've run has started.

Platform integration. Neon Blue sits inside your existing CRM — Braze, Klaviyo, or Iterable — and handles variant assignment and content delivery while your platform handles routing and sending. For on-site experiments, a lightweight JavaScript SDK connects to your website. Nothing gets replaced.

Channel audit and tracking. We map your current marketing touchpoints — lifecycle flows, push sequences, ad campaigns, key landing pages — and identify where testing will have the highest impact. We set up a shared tracking sheet as the single source of truth: what's running, what's planned, what we've learned.

2.2

Launching Your First Test

Once setup is complete, here's how a test goes from idea to live:

We propose the experiment. Based on our audit and your priorities, we present a test proposal: hypothesis, target flow, variant concepts, and expected timeline.

You review and approve. You review the proposal, give feedback on creative direction, and approve the test. This is your checkpoint to ensure everything aligns with brand and strategy.

We build and launch. We create the variants, set up the experiment in the relevant platform, configure audience splits, and launch. End-to-end technical implementation.

We monitor. Once live, we monitor for delivery issues, anomalies, and early signals. You don't need to babysit the test.

2.3

Test Results and Next Steps

Every test is followed up with results, key learnings, and next steps. These are provided in a clear, user-friendly report once the test reaches statistical significance — which we aim to achieve within two weeks.

Here’s what you’ll find in your test results:

Main Results Summary. What was the hypothesis? Was it confirmed or denied? And the headline KPI results — the quick read on what happened.

Performance Analysis. Performance by variant. Performance by segment. And any other segmentation that was made during the test — product, SKU, tone, angle, behavior, or whatever dimensions the experiment was designed around.

Insights. The most valuable part. Top learnings that came out of the test — this includes the hypothesis but is not limited to it. Our engine uncovers insights that weren’t necessarily planned for in the test setup. For each learning, you’ll see:

  • A summary of the learning
  • A graph representing the data that proves it
  • The next action required
  • Potential next steps to dig deeper into that learning

Executive Summary. A single view with the key learnings, action points, and next steps. Everything you need to share with your team or leadership — nothing you don’t.

3.1

Test Steps

Every experiment on Neon Blue follows the same five-step process:

  • Hypothesis: Define what you are testing and why you expect it to work.
  • Test Proposal: Translate the hypothesis into a concrete experiment plan with defined variables, audience, metrics, and timeline.
  • Creative Generation & Review: Generate the creative variants and review them together before anything goes live.
  • Launch: Build the experiment in your platform, QA everything, and go live.
  • Results & Next Steps: Analyze what happened, extract learnings, and define what comes next.

This cycle repeats. Each test builds on the last as learnings from one test feed the hypothesis for the next. The sections below walk through each step in detail.

3.2

Hypothesis

Every experiment starts with a hypothesis. A hypothesis is a structured, testable statement about what we expect to happen and why.

A good hypothesis has three parts:

Observation: What we’ve noticed in the data or the current creative. Examples: “The welcome email has a 15% open rate, well below benchmark”, “The retargeting ad has high impressions but almost no clicks.”

Theory: Why we think this is happening. “The subject line is generic and doesn’t give users a reason to open.” “The ad creative doesn’t match where the user is in their journey.”

Prediction: What we expect to change if we’re right. “A subject line that leads with the product’s core benefit will increase open rates by 5+ percentage points for these 3 user segments.”, “Benefit-focused ad copy tailored to the user’s last action will improve CTR by 15%+.”

Hypotheses force us to be specific before we see results. This prevents post-hoc rationalization and ensures every test produces a real learning in line with overall strategy. This makes the test as useful as possible, even when the hypothesis invalidated.

Hypotheses can come from your own knowledge and creativity or from Neon Blue’s AI analysing previous tests and performance.

3.3

Test Proposal

The test proposal is the blueprint for an experiment. It translates the hypothesis into a concrete plan that allows you to:

  • structure and visualize how the hypothesis will be tested
  • understand what the test is designed to learn and what the logical next tests could be
  • keep track of what has been tested and why

A test proposal includes:

  • Hypothesis: the statement defined in Section 3.2
  • Channel and touchpoint: the channel being tested — email, push, ads, or landing page — and the specific flow, message, campaign, or page involved
  • Variables being tested: the specific elements being isolated, such as user segment, angle, tone, copy, layout, timing, visual creative, page structure, or metadata
  • Variant concepts: what each version is designed to communicate and how the variants differ from one another
  • Audience and split: who sees the test and how traffic is divided across variants
  • Success metrics: the primary metric (for example, click-through rate) and secondary metrics (for example, conversion rate or unsubscribe rate)

This step comes before creative generation to ensure that every asset produced contributes to validating or invalidating the hypothesis.

3.4

Creative Generation & Review

Once the test has been defined in sufficient detail, creative generation can begin.

Creative generation is split into two parts: variants and generations.

A variant represents a distinct branch of the test. It captures a specific direction within the hypothesis being tested. A generation is a specific creative execution within that variant.

If the hypothesis is narrow, you may only need a single variant. For example, if you are testing simple copy personalization by persona, you might have one variant with one generation per persona.

If the hypothesis is broader, you may need multiple variants. For example, if you are testing personalization by segment across copy, intent, product focus, or messaging angle, the experiment may require several distinct variants to properly cover the test space.

For each variant, Neon Blue automatically generates a prompt based on the test proposal defined in Section 3.3. That prompt is then used to produce the individual generations within the variant.

Once generations are complete, you can refine them in two ways:

  • edit individual generations one by one using AI regeneration
  • edit the variant as a whole to regenerate a broader set of outputs
3.5

Launch

Once creative has been reviewed and approved, the experiment moves to launch. This step happens in your platform — your email tool, ad manager, CMS, or push provider — not inside Neon Blue.

Neon Blue's role here is to ensure nothing is missed before you go live. Before launching, QA your setup: confirm the variants are correctly assigned, targeting is correct, traffic splits match the proposal, success metrics are tracked, and a clear end date or significance threshold is defined.

A test that goes live with a broken tracking setup, an unintended audience overlap, or unequal traffic splits produces results you can't trust. The launch step is where discipline matters most.

3.6

Results & Next Steps

Once a test reaches statistical significance — or its predetermined end date — results are reviewed inside Neon Blue. The goal of the results step is not just to identify a winner, but to extract a documented learning.

A complete result includes:

  • A summary of the learning
  • The data that supports it
  • The next action or follow-on test it implies

Results that are not documented become forgotten. The learning library built through each test is what allows your creative programme to compound over time — each test informing the next hypothesis, each hypothesis sharpening your understanding of your audience.

Creative Variants Deep Dive

The art and science of building variants that produce real learnings — how to map concepts, cleanly isolate variables, and avoid common experimental pitfalls.

4.1

What Makes a Great Variant

Not all variants are created equal. A great variant is designed to isolate a specific question and answer it clearly. Here's what strong variants look like:

Each variant tests a distinct concept: Not minor word swaps — fundamentally different creative approaches. If you can't articulate what's different about this variant in one sentence, it's not different enough.

The variable is cleanly isolated: Everything except the thing being tested stays constant. If you change both the subject line and the CTA, you can't attribute the result to either one.

They span the range of possibilities: Good variant sets explore the full spectrum. If testing tone, include formal, conversational, and playful — not just two shades of formal.

They're grounded in strategy: Every variant maps back to a hypothesis. Random creative experiments produce random learnings.

4.2

Variant Examples

Here are the most common variables we test, roughly ordered by typical impact:

Message structure and strategy: The overall approach of the email. Benefit-led vs. feature-led vs. social-proof-driven. Single-CTA vs. multi-option. Long-form vs. concise. These are the biggest swings and often produce the biggest results.

Subject line and preview text: We test different framing (question vs. statement), value props, personalization (name, behavior, preference), and emotional register (urgency, curiosity, excitement).

Body copy and tone: How the message reads. Formal vs. casual. Instructional vs. inspirational. What sounds "right" to the marketing team doesn't always resonate with users.

Visual layout and design: Image-heavy vs. text-focused. Single column vs. multi-card. Hero image vs. inline graphics. Template changes vs. full redesigns.

Timing and cadence: When the message sends relative to a trigger event. Especially impactful in onboarding and reactivation flows.

4.3

Common Pitfalls

Even well-structured tests can fail to produce useful learnings if the experiment design is weak. The most common pitfalls are:

Testing too small: "Buy Now" vs. "Shop Now" is unlikely to teach you anything meaningful. Test different reasons to buy, not just different button labels.

Changing multiple things at once: If Variant B has a different subject line, hero image, and CTA — and wins — you won’t know which change actually mattered.

No control variant: Without a baseline, you can see which variant beat the others, but not whether any of them outperformed your current approach.

Wrong success metric: A subject line that lifts opens but tanks clicks is not a winner. Define primary and secondary metrics upfront.

Stopping too early: Small sample sizes produce unreliable results. Let experiments run until they reach significance before calling a winner.

No documentation: A result without context is just a number. Every experiment should produce a documented learning that future tests can build on.

4.4

From Variants to Learnings

The real value of creative variants isn't finding winners — it's building understanding. Over the course of a quarter, a structured program reveals patterns: your audience responds to social proof more than feature lists. They prefer concise messaging. Personalization in headlines lifts engagement but not always conversion. Video ads outperform static for acquisition but not retargeting. These patterns become your creative playbook.

And because Neon Blue tests across email, push, ads, and landing pages, insights from one channel compound across every other. A winning value proposition in email informs your ad creative. A high-converting landing page structure shapes your push notification strategy.

That's the endgame: not just better performance in one channel, but a deeper understanding of what makes your customers tick — everywhere you reach them.
