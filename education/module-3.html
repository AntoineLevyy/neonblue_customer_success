Experiments

The full lifecycle of a Neon Blue experiment — from hypothesis to results.

Section 3.1

Hypothesis

Every experiment starts with a hypothesis. Not a guess — a structured, testable statement about what we expect to happen and why.

A good hypothesis has three parts:

1

Observation

What we've noticed in the data or the current flow. "The welcome email has a 15% open rate, below benchmark."

2

Theory

Why we think this is happening. "The subject line is generic and doesn't communicate value."

3

Prediction

What we expect to change if we're right. "A subject line that leads with the product's core benefit will increase open rates by 5+ percentage points."

Why This Matters

Hypotheses force us to be specific about what we're testing and what success looks like before we see results. This prevents post-hoc rationalization and ensures every test produces a genuine learning — even when the result is negative.

Section 3.2

Test Proposal

The test proposal is the blueprint for an experiment. It translates the hypothesis into a concrete plan. You'll receive it for review before anything gets built — this is the approval checkpoint.

Hypothesis — The structured observation, theory, and prediction.

Flow and touchpoint — Which email or message in which flow we're testing.

Variable being tested — The specific element we're isolating (subject line, body copy, CTA, layout, timing, etc.)

Variant descriptions — What each version will look like and how they differ.

Audience and split — Who sees the test and how traffic is divided.

Success metrics — Primary (e.g., click-through rate) and secondary (e.g., conversion, unsubscribe) metrics.

Duration — How long we expect the test to run to reach significance.

Section 3.3

Creative Variants

This is where the experiment comes to life. We create the actual email or message variants that users will see. A few key principles guide how we design them:

Isolate one variable

If we're testing subject lines, the body copy stays constant. If we're testing layout, the copy stays constant. This ensures we know exactly what caused the difference.

Test meaningfully different concepts

We don't test "Buy Now" vs. "Shop Now." We test fundamentally different approaches. Bigger creative swings produce bigger learnings.

Always include a control

Every test includes the current version as a baseline. This tells us not just which variant won, but whether any variant outperformed what was already there.

Design for learning

The goal isn't to find a winner — it's to understand your audience better. Even a "losing" variant teaches us something valuable.

Section 3.4

Launch

Once variants are approved, we build and launch the experiment in your CRM platform.

1

Variants are built

Braze canvas paths, Iterable experiments, Klaviyo flows — we build inside your platform using your existing templates and brand assets.

2

Audience segments configured

We set up and validate the segment criteria to ensure the right users are entering the experiment.

3

Traffic splits set

Typically even splits across variants. We discuss and agree on split logic before launch.

4

QA performed

Every variant is reviewed for rendering, links, personalization, and deliverability before going live.

Your involvement at launch

Minimal. We handle the technical setup. You may be asked to confirm audience criteria or approve final creative renders.

Section 3.5

Test Results

When an experiment completes, we analyze and present the results. A results readout includes:

Performance by variant — Key metrics for each variant, clearly showing the winner and the magnitude of difference.

Statistical confidence — Whether the results are statistically significant or directional.

Key learning — The "so what" — what this result tells us about the audience, the flow, or the creative approach.

Recommended next step — Scale the winner, iterate further, or move to a new hypothesis.

Results don't live in a vacuum. Each experiment's learning informs the next round of tests. Over time, you build a compounding knowledge base about what resonates with your audience — and that knowledge becomes a durable competitive advantage.
